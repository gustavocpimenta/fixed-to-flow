Lisboa · 2036

Ten years from now, Lisbon will be a different city.
This afternoon, you live there.

A social simulation for 20 people. One question: who do we want to be?

Scroll

Lisbon is changing. Everyone feels it. Few talk about it honestly.

This is an invitation to do that. Not in a panel. Not in a workshop. In five scenarios set ten years from now, with twenty people who actually live here, mixing reactions and discomfort in real time.

You come in. You leave different.

“The emotions you feel during the simulation are not distractions. They are data.”

How the afternoon works

You join a team of four to five people. Portuguese and international residents, mixed on purpose. Over the course of the afternoon, your team receives five dispatches from 2036. Short scenarios. One paragraph each. They escalate in strangeness and stakes.

The five dispatches

Five futures. Each one possible.

These are fictional. The feelings they produce are not. Read them in order.

01

The vote that split the wrong way

Lisbon's first Mixed Neighborhood Council forms in Graça. Half the seats go to Portuguese residents, half to foreign residents. Their first vote bans Airbnb across the entire neighborhood. The ban passes. But the votes do not split the way anyone predicted. Some of the strongest voices for the ban come from foreign residents who have lived there for eight years. Some of the strongest opposition comes from Portuguese families who rent out a room to survive. The neighborhood is quieter now. Nobody is sure if that is good or bad.

02

The school that made a choice

The most sought-after primary school in Lisbon now has 60% of students with foreign-born parents. Portuguese is the third language on the playground. The principal made all classes bilingual. Test scores went up. Then came the letters. Half the Portuguese parents celebrating. Half pulling their children out. The teachers did not know which reaction was harder to sit with.

03

The belonging tax break

Portugal launches the Roots Visa. Live in Lisbon for over five years, show active community involvement, and you receive the same tax benefits as a Portuguese citizen. Demand explodes. Neighborhood associations that had been dying come back to life overnight. Then come the volunteering factories: foreign residents completing community hours for the visa without any real connection to the place. Nobody meant for it to happen. Nobody knows how to stop it.

04

The language that slipped away

A 2036 study finds that 40% of young people born in Lisbon to foreign parents do not speak Portuguese fluently. The government responds with mandatory Portuguese classes for all residents. The protest that follows is the largest Lisbon has seen since 2025. One sign reads: “You want us here, but you want us to be you.” Another sign, carried by a Portuguese woman: “Language is not a wall. It is a door.”

05

The city that changed the question

Lisbon wins the European Urban Integration Award. The jury notes that Lisbon is the only city in Southern Europe where the word “foreigner” no longer appears in municipal legislation. The new term is “first-generation residents.” The mayor, a Portuguese woman born to Angolan parents, gives one line in her acceptance speech: “We didn't solve the problem. We changed the question.” The audience stands. Not everyone cries. Most do.

“Joy signals what you want. Dread signals what you need to prepare for. Discomfort signals that real thinking is happening.”

Facilitator Guide

The Emotional Arc

What participants feel at each moment of the afternoon. Use this to anticipate the room, calibrate your facilitation, and know when to hold space.

2:30 PM · Arrival

Nervous energy, polite distance

Participants are scanning the room. They do not know each other. Expect small talk as armor. The mix of Portuguese and foreign voices creates curiosity but also hesitation. Handing them a notebook and pen gives them something to hold. It grounds them. Watch for anyone who isolates. A quiet word from a host brings them in.

3:30 PM · First Dispatch

Shock of the format

The first reading lands and the room goes silent. Some write immediately, others freeze. Both are valid. The three minutes of silence after reading is the most important moment of the entire afternoon. Do not rush it. Do not fill it. Watch for pens moving. That is the pre-feeling activating. The team discussion that follows will feel cautious. That is normal. Trust the escalation.

4:40 PM · Third Dispatch

Defenses come down

By now, the polite distance is gone. Watch for the moment someone says something they did not plan to say. That is the signal the real work has begun. Tables will get louder. Some people will get quieter. Both mean it is working. The mixed teams matter most here: Portuguese and international residents will have visibly different reactions to the same scenario. Do not smooth over the tension. That tension is the point.

5:15 PM · Break

Processing in the open air

Do not underestimate this moment. The garden conversations are where people process what just happened. Cross-team exchanges start naturally. Some participants will need to be alone. Let them. Others will cluster and talk urgently. The break is part of the emotional arc, not a pause from it. Resist the impulse to start Phase 2 early.

6:05 PM · Final Dispatch

Quiet, not energy

The room is emotionally different from three hours ago. Expect quiet, not applause. The lack of visible energy is a sign it is working. People are sitting with what they feel instead of rushing to respond. Notebooks are full. Some participants will look surprised by their own handwriting. Hold the silence after the final share-out longer than feels comfortable. That silence is where the afternoon lands.

8:00 PM · Dinner

The real conversations begin

The formal structure is gone. Watch for new connections forming between people from different teams. Notebooks on the table signal people are still processing. The most honest conversations of the day happen now. Someone will learn another person's name for the first time, four hours in. Do not close the evening. Let it close itself.

If participants leave still thinking about the afternoon the next morning, the facilitation worked.

Organizers Only

You are now in the back office. What follows is for organizers only: format rationale, methodology notes, logistics, and roles. None of this is shared with guests.

Methodology: What We Are Actually Doing

This format synthesizes techniques from Jane McGonigal's Imaginable into a single facilitated afternoon. It is important to be honest about this: McGonigal presents these techniques as a cumulative progression across her book, not as a single-event format. Her large-scale IFTF games (Superstruct, EVOKE) were multi-week online experiences, not afternoon workshops.

What we do here is an adaptation faithful to the spirit and science of her work, not a replication of a format she runs. We can honestly say this is "inspired by the Imaginable methodology" and "based on futures thinking principles from Jane McGonigal."

Techniques Embedded in the Format

Episodic Future Thinking (EFT)

Vividly pre-experiencing a future moment using the same neural systems that reconstruct memories. Not analysis from the outside. Immersive simulation from the inside.

Every dispatch. The 4 grounding questions.

Pre-Feeling

The real emotional response generated during future simulations. Joy signals what you want. Dread signals what you need to prepare for. These feelings are data, not distraction.

Silent individual reaction (3 min after each dispatch).

Hard Empathy

Deliberate effort to understand lives radically unlike your own. Goes beyond ordinary empathy (relating through shared experience) into territory that requires real work.

"Who is most affected?" question. Mixed teams.

Signals of Change

Real, concrete, observable examples of something already happening in the world that suggests things are shifting. Small in scale, often surprising, always verifiable.

"What signal from today connects?" question.

Urgent Optimism

Not passive hope ("things will be fine") but active, felt agency ("things might be hard, and I can respond"). A trainable psychological posture, not a personality trait.

"What do you want to do about it?" question.

Shadow + Positive Imagination

Two deliberate modes of thinking, both essential. Positive imagination builds confidence and reduces paralysis. Shadow imagination builds readiness and prevents denial. Realistic hope is the balance.

Dispatches alternate between hopeful and threatening.

Dator's Law

"Any useful statement about the future should, at first, seem ridiculous." If a scenario does not feel strange, it is just an extrapolation of the present, not genuinely useful for building readiness.

Dispatches 4 and 5 push into "ridiculous" territory.

Call to Adventure

Shifts the experience from threat response to opportunity detection. Moves people from "that was interesting" to "here is what I will do." Produces urgent optimism rather than anxiety.

Phase 4 personal reflection.

Why the Mixed Room Changes Everything

In most futures exercises, Hard Empathy is simulated. You ask someone to imagine being in another person's shoes. Here, the other person is sitting next to them. On the same team.

When a Portuguese participant hears Dispatch 4 ("The Language That Was Lost") and the foreign resident next to them has a completely different reaction, that is not an exercise. That is real emotional information. The pre-feeling is genuine. The discomfort is genuine. That is the point.

Event Flow

Phase 1: Arrival and Context (30 min) Garden

Guests arrive. Coffee. Informal conversation. Each person receives a notebook and pen.

Brief opening by the three organizers (5 minutes each, no slides):

  • Graça: Why thinking about the future matters. Futura Foundation's mission. The concept of "pre-feeling" futures before they arrive.
  • Gus: What a social simulation is. The rules: no right answers. Emotions are data, not weakness. The goal is to prepare, not predict.
  • Nuno: Look Lisbon as a meeting place. Why this neighborhood, this city, this conversation.

Form teams of 4-5. Deliberate mix: Portuguese and international residents in every team.

Phase 2: The Five Rounds (3 hours) Auditorium

Chairs in clusters of 4-5, not rows. Each round follows the same structure (~35 min):

  1. Dispatch read aloud (2 min). Read by one of the organizers.
  2. Silent individual reaction (3 min). Write in notebook: first reaction, what do I feel?
  3. Team discussion (20 min). Four guiding questions printed on each table.
  4. Brief share-out (10 min). One spokesperson per team. 2 min each. No debate. Just listen.

15-minute break between dispatches 3 and 4. Garden. Fresh air. The informal conversations during the break are part of the process.

Phase 3: Synthesis (45 min) Auditorium

Co-facilitated by Gus and Graça.

  • What patterns emerged across the five dispatches?
  • What surprised you about your own reactions?
  • Where did teams diverge the most?
  • What signals from the present came up repeatedly?

This phase does not seek consensus. It seeks patterns and surprises.

Phase 4: Personal Reflection (30 min) Garden

Individual (15 min): "After living this afternoon in 2036, what is one thing I want to change about how I am living in 2026?" Write in the notebook. Be specific.

Pairs (15 min): Share with one person. Not the whole team. Intimacy. Someone who listens and witnesses.

Dinner (open-ended) Garden

Dinner is part of the event, not an afterthought. The conversations during dinner are often the most honest ones. Notebooks stay on the table.

Experience Design

Signature Elements

  • The notebook: Every participant writes by hand throughout. Their notebook becomes a personal artifact of the afternoon.
  • The mixed teams: Portuguese and international residents on every team. The diversity is not a bonus. It is the mechanism.
  • The escalation arc: Five dispatches that move from plausible to strange. By dispatch 4, the room feels different than it did at dispatch 1.
  • Dinner as resolution: The event does not end with a closing speech. It ends with food, wine, and the most honest conversations of the day.

EPIC Framework Analysis

From The Power of Moments (Chip & Dan Heath). Which elements create peak moments in this experience?

Elevation

The entire format breaks routine. Stepping into 2036, receiving "dispatches from the future," the escalating strangeness of the scenarios. No screens, no slides. An afternoon unlike anything else.

Pride

Participants are invited because they are trusted to engage with difficult questions. The personal reflection ("what do I want to change about how I am living in 2026?") honors the courage to look honestly at oneself.

Insight

The dispatches are designed to surface surprising reactions. "Not the way anyone expected" is a recurring motif. The synthesis phase explicitly looks for patterns and surprises. The mixed room guarantees that someone next to you sees the same future differently.

Connection

Five people on a team, debating futures that matter to all of them. The pair sharing in Phase 4 creates intimacy. Dinner continues the conversation. These are bonds forged through shared emotional experience, not small talk.

Post-Event Follow-Up

  1. Thank-you message to participants (within 48 hours). Personal, not templated. Include photos from the event.
  2. Feedback request (sent with or shortly after the thank-you). Short. Not a survey. 2-3 open questions: What stayed with you? What would you change? Would you come back for a different theme?
  3. Organizer debrief (within 1 week). Graça, Gus, Nuno. What worked, what to change, does the format continue? Decide: standalone, part of Time Machine, different theme, pause.
  4. Written piece capturing key patterns and insights from the event. Co-authored or co-signed by the three organizers. Shared with participants first, then published (newsletter, LinkedIn, or similar).
