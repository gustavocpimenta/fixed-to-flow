Section 1

Business Context

Vitolero is an AI-powered cigar companion app. Photo-first identification, pairings, and social sharing, delivered as a Progressive Web App. No App Store dependency.

The core value proposition (v4.0, April 17 pivot): turn any cigar photo into a magazine-quality share with brand and line auto-identified. Cigar-specific photo filters desaturate the background, keep the cigar saturated, and overlay brand and line information on the photo. The filter is what makes the photo worth sharing. The identification is what makes the share credible.

MVP scope (3 features): Snap & Share with filters, one-directional Pairing (cigar to 3 food + 3 drink), and Journal (Pinterest-style gallery, account creation on first Journal access).

Deferred to post-MVP: Signature Taste Profile, In-store Recommendation, Discovery Feed, bi-directional Pairing, menu-aware Pairing, Cigar Doctor, Photo Import, vitola-level identification.

The core constraint: paid advertising is structurally impossible. EU tobacco directive, platform restrictions across Meta, Google, and TikTok, and Apple's history of banning cigar apps close every conventional channel. The product must grow itself through organic shares and Facebook-group admin partnerships (outreach begins only once MVP is working).

This document is the post April 17 pivot concept framework: personas, principles, interaction model, features, flows, growth strategy, and social network channel strategy. A compass, not a blueprint.

Platform Constraints (v1)
Constraint

Smartphones only. Target phones exclusively. Exclude tablets and desktop. Cigar use cases are phone scenarios.

Constraint

No offline support. AI-driven features require internet. Internet connectivity is a stated assumption for v1.

Constraint

PWA push notification limits. Particularly on iOS. Social sign-in as partial mitigation for alternate notification channels.

Constraint

No cigar authentication. Verifying whether a cigar is genuine is not yet technically feasible. Removed from v1 scope.

Section 2

Strategy & Growth

How Vitolero grows without paid advertising, how it makes money, and what metrics matter.

The Distribution Problem
Blocked

EU tobacco directive. EUTPD2 bans tobacco advertising across the bloc.

Blocked

Platform ad restrictions. Meta, Google, TikTok all block tobacco-related paid content.

Blocked

App Store risk. Apple removed Cigar Scanner for "promoting tobacco." Native apps are vulnerable.

Blocked

VC reluctance. Pre-seed "sin industry" funding is near-impossible without traction proof.

Answer

Value first, share after. The app delivers identification and personalized recommendations. Users stay because the app learns their palate. The share card rides on top of this value: after every identification, the user is prompted to share. Every shared card carries the Vitolero domain. Organic growth through behavior people already do.

Priority

Growth is the only goal for v1. Monetization and data flywheels are secondary concerns. The singular focus is getting users in, getting them to snap, and getting the share loop spinning.

Two-Speed Growth Flywheel (April 17 MVP)

Front wheel (MVP, high-speed): Photo filter + brand and line identification + share. Every snap produces a share-worthy artifact. The share prompt is the primary action after identification. This front wheel must be perfect because every user passes through it.

Back wheel (post-MVP, retention): Journal holds the raw photo and brand data in MVP. Post-MVP, the Signature Taste Profile, Discovery Feed, and recommendations ship on top of that data. Switching cost begins accruing once recommendations land.

Step 1
User snaps a cigar
Step 2
App identifies cigar, delivers value (flavor profile, strength, recommendations)
Step 3
User saves to profile. Taste profile updates.
Step 4

Step 5

Step 6
Friends see the card with Vitolero domain
Step 7
Curiosity drives a new download
Loop repeats. Value first, share after. Each user is a distribution channel.
Monetization Phases
Phase 1

Free Launch

Everything available, no limitations, no trial periods. The goal is to attract users, build the community, and observe real behavior at scale.

Signal to advance: consistent weekly active users and identifiable usage patterns.
Phase 2

Observe

Which features drive return visits? What creates switching costs? The taste profile is the primary candidate: 30+ cigars logged means the app knows their palate. That data does not transfer.

Signal to advance: clear value differentiation between casual and power usage patterns.
Phase 3

Paid Tier

Introduce based on observed value, not assumptions. Pricing starts at $2.99 to $4.99 per month. Trivial for someone spending $800+ per year on cigars.

Premium candidates: advanced analytics, AI pairing, collection export, priority identification.
Data Flywheel

User Data (per snap)

What every interaction generates
  • Cigar identification (brand, line, vitola)
  • Timestamp and location
  • Rating (optional)
  • Tasting notes (enthusiast users)
  • Pairing data
  • Labeled training pair for ML

Platform Intelligence

What the data builds over time
  • Personal taste profiles
  • Recommendation engine
  • Community ratings and consensus
  • ML training corpus for band recognition
  • Pairing correlation data
  • Smoking pattern analytics

Photo import accelerates this flywheel. Users who bring existing cigar photos from their camera roll or Facebook history build a taste profile in minutes instead of weeks. Every imported photo also generates a labeled training pair.

Section 5

Jobs to Be Done

Five universal jobs shared by every persona. Eight persona-specific jobs (including Dimitar's reformulations). Identify is the entry point to value. Share is the entry point to growth.

Universal Jobs

Identify

"Tell me what this is."
Photo in, identification out. The first value delivered. The universal entry point for every persona at every stage.
Ritualist Curiosity. "What's this cigar my friend gave me?"
Enthusiast Validation. Tests if the app gets it right before trusting it.
Occasional Confidence. "What am I smoking?"
Newcomer First contact. The cigar in their hand is the only data they have.
Connoisseur Rare use. Already knows by sight, but useful for unfamiliar boutique blends.

Choose

"Help me choose."
The in-store recommendation job. Contextual: user has options in front of them and needs guidance.
Ritualist "Something good" from whatever's in the shop. Low friction, clear winner.
Enthusiast "Something I haven't tried that matches my profile but excludes what I dislike."
Occasional "What's impressive for a dinner tonight?" Occasion and social context drive the query.
Newcomer "Just tell me which one to buy." Paralyzed by choice.

Discover

"Find great cigars for me."
The exploration job. Not contextual: user wants to expand their world beyond what they already know. Drives retention.
Enthusiast "Show me something I haven't seen." Discovery is the primary retention driver.
Ritualist "What else would I like?" Expands the ritual with new finds.
Connoisseur "Show me something I haven't seen." The hardest query to satisfy.

Remember

"Remember what I like."
No persona wants to re-explain preferences. The Signature Taste Profile is the hidden engine behind this job. Built from snap behavior, never from forms.
Ritualist Will not rate or log. Profile must build from photos and saves.
Enthusiast Will actively rate, note, and track. Wants to see their profile evolve.
Occasional Low input. Remembers occasions, not flavor notes.
Newcomer Has no preferences yet. The app must detect them before the user can articulate them.
Connoisseur Has a spreadsheet. Will import or replace it if the app proves better.

Share

"Show what I'm smoking."
The distribution engine. Prompted after value delivery, not before. Users share because the card makes them look knowledgeable.
Ritualist Shares as-is. Zero friction. The card is the moment captured.
Enthusiast Adds tasting notes. The card reflects genuine expertise.
Occasional The card IS the value. Answers "what are you smoking?" for him.
Newcomer Learns from the card. Share card is education.

Key Decision Value first, share after

The core proposition is "Snap 3 cigars to unlock your personalized flavor profile and next-buy recommendations." Identification and profile building are the value. Sharing is the distribution byproduct.

Key Decision Signature Taste Profile replaces Collection/Humidor

Hidden engine behind Discovery and Recommendations. Not surfaced to users: (1) proprietary advantage, (2) may conflict with user self-perception of their taste.

Killed Cigar Doctor

Community research confirms cigar damage/defect questions are infrequent. Strong candidate for a future premium tier.

Merged Pairing

"Cigar to drink" and "drink to cigar" are the same feature from the user's perspective. One unified pairing feature.

Section 7

Design Principles

Six testable principles. Each includes its own failure condition. Plus operational implications and the two-mode interaction model.

Layer 1: Six Principles
Principle 1

Camera is the front door.

Every core task starts with a photo. The camera is not a feature inside the app. It IS the app's primary interface.

Failure: A user has to navigate to find a camera.
Principle 2

Perceived value in 10 seconds.

The sommelier narrative IS the first result. Within 1 to 2 seconds of capture, the user sees the AI analyzing their photo. "I see a dark wrapper with an oily sheen..." delivers perceived value before the final identification lands. No wizard, no account creation, no feature tour. The user feels the app working the moment they tap the shutter.

Failure: If the user sees nothing happening for more than 2 seconds after capture, the design failed.
Principle 3

The app reads you.

Profile building happens passively from behavior: what you photograph, what you ask about, what you return to, what you skip. The app detects the interaction type and adapts the interface accordingly. Active input exists for enthusiasts but is never required.

Failure: A casual user must actively configure preferences for good recommendations.
Principle 4

One answer, scaled to depth.

The AI knows a lot. The interface must not get in the way of surfacing that intelligence. The same query produces different output depth depending on who's asking. A newbie gets "try this one." An enthusiast gets blend details, flavor notes, and comparative context.

Failure: All users see the same output regardless of expertise.
Principle 5

Companion over time, tool in the moment.

Two interaction patterns coexist. Quick Mode for "right now" decisions. Reflective Mode for "at home" activities.

Failure: The app forces reflective engagement on someone who wants a quick answer.
Principle 06

Every photo is marketing.

The share card is the growth engine. The product must make sharing feel natural, not transactional. No CTAs, no "download our app." The domain does the attribution.

Failure: If the share card doesn't look like something a cigar magazine produced, the design failed.
Layer 2: Design Implications
  • Output depth scales with the user, not the feature. The same identification result shows different levels of detail by persona.
  • First Encounter must deliver perceived value in under 10 seconds. The sommelier narrative begins within 1 to 2 seconds of capture, giving the user immediate feedback. Onboarding happens after the first result, not before.
  • The app needs two modes, not six personas. Jobs cluster into two interaction patterns (Quick and Reflective), not six separate persona experiences.
  • Passive profile building is non-negotiable. Most personas will not actively rate, log, or journal. The app must build profiles from behavior.
  • Churn follows a predictable pattern. First Encounter and Early Use are the danger zones. All retention investment goes to these two stages.
Layer 3: Two Modes
bolt

Quick Mode

In the moment, at the shop, at the lounge
  • Identify this cigar
  • Recommend from what's here
  • Pair with this drink
  • Snap and share
Camera-first. All personas.
auto_stories

Reflective Mode

At home, between smokes
  • Browse recommendations
  • Manage collection
  • Review past smokes
  • Track history
List/card-based. Enthusiasts, Connoisseurs.

Context-based switching. The app infers which mode from behavior. Camera open equals Quick. Scrolling diary equals Reflective. The mode is a design lens, not a user-facing concept.

Open Question

Mode switching needs concrete definition. What signals trigger the switch? What is the fallback when inference is ambiguous? (Presumably Quick Mode.) Should users have an explicit toggle? Needs resolution before Call 3.

Section 8

User Flow Sketches

Two flows that demonstrate the app's grammar. The hero flow shows the growth engine in action. The second flow shows the core utility loop.

photo_camera

Flow 1: Snap & Share (Hero Flow)

Dave (Weekend Ritualist) is on his back porch on a Saturday evening with a cigar and bourbon. He wants to capture the moment.
1
Camera State
Dave opens the app. The camera is already active. He points it at the cigar in his hand.
2
Capture
He taps the shutter. The app captures a close-up of the cigar with the band visible.
3
Processing State
"I see a dark wrapper with an oily sheen... The band suggests a Padron family... Checking the vitola proportions..."
4
Result: Identification
"Padron 3000 Maduro. Full-bodied with chocolate and earth. Nicaragua." The result card appears with brand, line, vitola, flavor, strength.
5
Save to Profile
Dave taps "Save to My Humidor." The cigar is logged. Profile updates: "You lean toward Nicaraguan Maduros. Save 2 more to unlock AI recommendations."
6
Share Prompt (After Value)
"Looks like a great smoke. Generate a share card?" A branded card appears: the photo with a clean overlay. Looks like something from a cigar magazine.
7
Share to Facebook
Dave taps Share. The card goes to Facebook. No CTA, no "download our app." The domain on the card does the attribution. Three friends ask where the card came from.

Why this flow matters

Value first, share after. Dave got identification, flavor profile, and a profile update before being asked to share. He shared because the card makes him look knowledgeable, not because the app told him to. Three friends now know about the app. Every snap builds the profile. Every share is distribution.

storefront

Flow 2: In-Store Recommendation

Carlos (Deepening Enthusiast) is at his local cigar shop. He wants something that matches his taste but tries something new.
1
Camera State
Carlos opens the app. The camera is already active. He points it at the humidor glass display.
2
Capture
He taps the shutter. The app captures a wide shot of the shelf showing about 12 different cigars.
3
Processing State
"I can see about 12 different cigars on this shelf... Identifying the Arturo Fuente in the top row... The dark wrapper on the second shelf looks like a Padron..."
4
Result: Recommendation
#1 pick: "Padron 3000 Maduro. 92% match. Full-bodied with chocolate and earth. Similar to the Oliva Serie V you saved last month." Four more options below, accounting for his dislikes.
5
Detail View
Taps the top card. Full details: flavor profile, strength, smoke time, price range. "Save to collection" and "Pair with a drink?" prompts.
6
Optional: Pairing
"Try this with a bourbon. The sweetness complements the chocolate notes." Could also photograph the bar's drink menu.
7
Save and Exit
Saves the recommendation, buys the cigar. Total interaction: under 90 seconds.

Why this flow matters

This is the core utility loop. Carlos came because the app respects what he dislikes, not just what he likes. The recommendation precision is what keeps enthusiasts coming back.

Why these two flows

The hero flow shows the growth engine: every snap becomes marketing, every share becomes distribution. The utility flow shows the retention loop: precise recommendations that account for individual taste. Together they demonstrate the app's grammar. Camera in, intelligence out, profile builds silently, sharing happens naturally.

Note on onboarding

Previous v1 included a dedicated onboarding flow. In v2, onboarding is folded into the Snap & Share hero flow. The first snap IS onboarding. Value delivery and profile building happen simultaneously. No separate entry sequence needed.
