Concept Framework

Section 1

Business Context

Vitolero is an AI-powered cigar companion app. Photo-first identification, pairings, and social sharing, delivered as a Progressive Web App. No App Store dependency.

The core value proposition: turn any cigar photo into a magazine-quality share with brand and line auto-identified. Each of the 3 cigar-specific filters applies a soft peripheral blur (bokeh) that keeps the cigar sharp while the background softens, then composites a brand overlay (logo, brand name, line, subtle Vitolero mark) as a strip across the bottom. The overlay is on by default and toggles off with a tap; the original photo is always preserved. The filter is what makes the photo worth sharing. The identification is what makes the share credible.

MVP scope (3 features): Snap & Share with filters, one-directional Pairing (cigar to 3 food + 3 drink), and Journal (Pinterest-style masonry gallery, account creation on first Journal access). A Cigar Detail screen and a PWA install prompt support the loop. Identification runs at three levels: brand and line surface fast for the share loop; specific size (vitola) surfaces later in the Cigar Detail screen.

Deferred to post-MVP: Signature Taste Profile, In-store Recommendation, Discovery Feed, bi-directional Pairing, menu-aware Pairing, Cigar Doctor, Photo Import, full vitola identification.

The core constraint: paid advertising is structurally impossible. EU tobacco directive, platform restrictions across Meta, Google, and TikTok, and Apple's history of banning cigar apps close every conventional channel. The product must grow itself through organic shares and Facebook-group admin partnerships (outreach begins only once MVP is working).

This concept framework covers: personas, principles, interaction model, features, flows, growth strategy, and social network channel strategy. A compass, not a blueprint.

Platform Constraints
Constraint

Smartphones only. Target phones exclusively. Exclude tablets and desktop. Cigar use cases are phone scenarios.

Constraint

No offline support. AI-driven features require internet. Internet connectivity is a stated assumption for v1.

Constraint

PWA push notification limits. Particularly on iOS. Social sign-in as partial mitigation for alternate notification channels.

Constraint

No cigar authentication. Verifying whether a cigar is genuine is not yet technically feasible. Removed from v1 scope.

Constraint

No Facebook API. Meta's tobacco restrictions block the Facebook API. Distribution uses deep links only. Each share is a separate action. No simultaneous multi-platform sharing.

Constraint

No post-share reacquisition control. Once the user taps share, they leave the app with no callback. The team is investigating reacquisition mechanisms (Fabio to follow up).

Constraint

Variable gallery aspect ratios. Photos arriving from the gallery come in portrait, landscape, and square. The interface handles all three without forced cropping.

Section 2

Strategy & Growth

How Vitolero grows without paid advertising, how it makes money, and what metrics matter.

The Distribution Problem
Blocked

EU tobacco directive. EUTPD2 bans tobacco advertising across the bloc.

Blocked

Platform ad restrictions. Meta, Google, TikTok all block tobacco-related paid content.

Blocked

App Store risk. Apple removed Cigar Scanner for "promoting tobacco." Native apps are vulnerable.

Blocked

VC reluctance. Pre-seed "sin industry" funding is near-impossible without traction proof.

Answer

The filter is the hook. A great-looking share is the first move, not a profile setup. After capture, the cigar-specific bokeh filter and brand overlay make the photo worth sharing, and identification makes the share credible. Every shared card carries the Vitolero domain into communities the app could never buy its way into. Organic growth through behavior people already do.

Priority

Growth is the only goal for v1. Monetization and data flywheels are secondary concerns. The singular focus is getting users in, getting them to snap, and getting the share loop spinning.

Two-Speed Growth Flywheel

Front wheel (MVP, high-speed): Photo filter + brand and line identification + share. Every snap produces a share-worthy artifact. The share prompt is the primary action after identification. This front wheel must be perfect because every user passes through it.

Back wheel (post-MVP, retention): Journal holds the raw photo and brand data in MVP. Post-MVP, the Signature Taste Profile, Discovery Feed, and recommendations ship on top of that data. Switching cost begins accruing once recommendations land.

Loop repeats. The filter is the hook, the share is the engine. Each user is a distribution channel.
Monetization Phases
Phase 1

Free Launch

Everything available, no limitations, no trial periods. The goal is to attract users, build the community, and observe real behavior at scale.

Signal to advance: consistent weekly active users and identifiable usage patterns.
Phase 2

Observe

Which features drive return visits? What creates switching costs? The taste profile is the primary candidate: 30+ cigars logged means the app knows their palate. That data does not transfer.

Signal to advance: clear value differentiation between casual and power usage patterns.
Phase 3

Paid Tier

Introduce based on observed value, not assumptions. Pricing starts at $2.99 to $4.99 per month. Trivial for someone spending $800+ per year on cigars.

Premium candidates: advanced analytics, AI pairing, collection export, priority identification.

Section 3

Personas

Six user types from community research across 240k+ members. Kept for long-term product thinking. Click any card for details.

MVP priority: Dave (Weekend Ritualist) is the sole MVP-primary persona. He is US-based, smokes alone on weekends with bourbon, and already photographs and shares cigar moments on Facebook. The filter-powered share loop maps directly onto behavior he already has.

Served post-MVP: Carlos, Mark, Tyler, Jerome, and Richard. Carlos carries a specific caveat: brand and line identification accuracy on well-known cigars is reputation-critical in MVP because he will find the app through Facebook groups and judge it publicly.

Operational principle: build for Dave, design for Dave's Saturday-night solo smoke.

Section 5

Jobs to Be Done

Seven jobs span the personas. The MVP serves Identify, Enhance via filter, Share, and Pair directly, with Remember partly covered by the Journal. Choose, Discover, and the full taste profile are post-MVP. Identify is the entry point to value. Share is the entry point to growth.

Universal Jobs

Identify MVP

"Tell me what this is."
Photo in, identification out. The first value delivered. Brand and line in MVP; vitola surfaces in the Cigar Detail screen. The universal entry point for every persona at every stage.
Ritualist Curiosity. "What's this cigar my friend gave me?"
Enthusiast Validation. Tests if the app gets it right before trusting it.
Occasional Confidence. "What am I smoking?"
Newcomer First contact. The cigar in their hand is the only data they have.
Connoisseur Rare use. Already knows by sight, but useful for unfamiliar boutique blends.

Enhance via filter MVP

"Make my photo look worth sharing."
The hero job. A cigar-specific bokeh filter and brand overlay turn a phone snapshot into a magazine-quality card. No skill required from the user.
Ritualist The reason the in-app share beats the native camera. Accepts the default filter.
Occasional The card is the value. The filter makes it look effortless.
Newcomer Looks knowledgeable without knowing the vocabulary.

Choose Post-MVP

"Help me choose."
The in-store recommendation job (post-MVP, needs the taste profile). Contextual: user has options in front of them and needs guidance.
Ritualist "Something good" from whatever's in the shop. Low friction, clear winner.
Enthusiast "Something I haven't tried that matches my profile but excludes what I dislike."
Occasional "What's impressive for a dinner tonight?" Occasion and social context drive the query.
Newcomer "Just tell me which one to buy." Paralyzed by choice.

Discover Post-MVP

"Find great cigars for me."
The exploration job (post-MVP, needs the taste profile). Not contextual: user wants to expand their world beyond what they already know. Drives retention.
Enthusiast "Show me something I haven't seen." Discovery is the primary retention driver.
Ritualist "What else would I like?" Expands the ritual with new finds.
Connoisseur "Show me something I haven't seen." The hardest query to satisfy.

Remember MVP: Journal profile post-MVP

"Remember what I like."
The Journal keeps a visual record in MVP. The Signature Taste Profile (post-MVP) is the hidden engine that later turns that history into recommendations. Built from snap behavior, never from forms.
Ritualist Will not rate or log. Profile must build from photos and saves.
Enthusiast Will actively rate, note, and track. Wants to see their profile evolve.
Occasional Low input. Remembers occasions, not flavor notes.
Newcomer Has no preferences yet. The app must detect them before the user can articulate them.
Connoisseur Has a spreadsheet. Will import or replace it if the app proves better.

Pair MVP

"What goes with this cigar?"
One-directional: cigar line to 3 food and 3 drink suggestions, one featured. A cigar-beside-list, text-only result with item names only. The next value layer after sharing.
Ritualist Most frequent pairing scenario. "What goes with the bourbon I just poured?"
Occasional A pairing makes the evening feel complete.
Newcomer Guidance without a vocabulary barrier.

Share MVP

"Show what I'm smoking."
The distribution engine. Prompted after the filter is applied and identification is shown. Users share because the card makes them look knowledgeable.
Ritualist Shares as-is. Zero friction. The card is the moment captured.
Enthusiast Adds tasting notes. The card reflects genuine expertise.
Occasional The card IS the value. Answers "what are you smoking?" for him.
Newcomer Learns from the card. Share card is education.

Key Decision Share-first, the filter is the hook

The first move is a great-looking share, not a profile setup. The bokeh filter and brand overlay make the photo worth sharing. Identification makes the share credible. No "snap 3 cigars to unlock." No wizard before value.

Key Decision Three features, everything else deferred

Snap & Share, Pairing, and Journal, supported by a Cigar Detail screen and a PWA install prompt. Taste profile, recommendations, and discovery wait for post-MVP. A pre-seed team ships a launchable product, not a feature-complete one.

Key Decision Account creation deferred to Journal

Snap, filter, identify, share, and pair all work with no account. The first tap on the Journal triggers a one-tap Google or Apple sign-in. The wall arrives only when there is something worth saving.

Key Decision Pairing locked: cigar beside the list, one featured, text-only

A big vertical cigar beside the 6 suggestions, item names only (no category tags or icons), Food and Drink dividers de-emphasized. Exactly one featured pairing per result. Text-only, because the visual budget is spent on the share card and speed matters in the 10 to 15 second budget. One-directional in MVP.

Killed Cigar Doctor

Community research confirms cigar damage and defect questions are infrequent. Strong candidate for a future premium tier.

Section 7

Design Principles

Six testable principles. Each includes its own failure condition. Plus operational implications and the two-mode interaction model.

Layer 1: Six Principles
Principle 1

Camera is the front door.

Every core task starts with a photo. The camera is not a feature inside the app. It IS the app's primary interface.

Failure: A user has to navigate to find a camera.
Principle 2

Perceived value in 10 seconds.

The filter result is visible almost immediately, before identification completes. A brief narrative runs during processing ("I see a dark wrapper with a gold band... Checking the database...") and the brand-plus-line result lands within 10 seconds. The heavy sommelier narrative from v3 is scaled back: brand-plus-line is fast, and the overlay now tells the story the narrative used to carry. The point is that the user sees something happening from the first moment.

Failure: If the user sees nothing happening for more than 2 seconds after capture, the design failed.
Principle 3

The app reads you.

In MVP, "reading you" means the returning user opens to their last photo, not a blank camera. The app remembers what you were doing. Full profile building is post-MVP, but the principle of continuity applies from day one.

Failure: The app treats every session like a fresh start.
Principle 4

One answer, scaled to depth.

The AI knows a lot. The interface must not get in the way of surfacing that intelligence. The same query produces different output depth depending on who's asking. A newbie gets "try this one." An enthusiast gets blend details, flavor notes, and comparative context.

Failure: All users see the same output regardless of expertise.
Principle 5

Companion over time, tool in the moment.

Two interaction patterns coexist. Quick Mode for "right now" decisions. Reflective Mode for "at home" activities.

Failure: The app forces reflective engagement on someone who wants a quick answer.
Principle 6

Every photo is marketing.

The filter is the growth engine. The product must make sharing feel natural, not transactional. No CTAs, no "download our app." The domain does the attribution. The filtered photo with the brand overlay should look like something a cigar magazine produced, not a screenshot of an app.

Failure: If the share card doesn't look like something a cigar magazine produced, the design failed.
Layer 2: Design Implications
  • Output depth scales with the user, not the feature. The same identification result shows different levels of detail by persona.
  • First Encounter must deliver perceived value in under 10 seconds. The filter result shows almost immediately and a brief narrative covers the brand-plus-line identification. Onboarding happens after the first result, not before.
  • The app needs two modes, not six personas. Jobs cluster into two interaction patterns (Quick and Reflective), not six separate persona experiences.
  • Returning users get continuity. The app opens to the last photo taken, not a blank camera. Deep profile building is post-MVP, but continuity applies from day one.
  • Churn follows a predictable pattern. First Encounter and Early Use are the danger zones. All retention investment goes to these two stages.
Layer 3: Two Modes
bolt

Quick Mode

On the porch, at the lounge, in the moment
  • Snap and share
  • Apply a filter
  • Identify this cigar
  • Pair with food and drink
Camera-first. All personas.
auto_stories

Reflective Mode

At home, between smokes
  • Browse the Journal
  • Open Cigar Detail
  • Favorite past smokes
  • Review history
Journal-based. Dave browsing, Carlos going deeper.

Context-based switching. The app infers which mode from behavior. Camera open equals Quick. Scrolling diary equals Reflective. The mode is a design lens, not a user-facing concept.

Mode is inferred, not chosen. Camera open is Quick. Browsing the Journal is Reflective. There is no user-facing toggle in MVP, and when inference is ambiguous the app defaults to Quick.

Section 9

User Flow Sketches

Three flows that demonstrate the app's grammar. The hero flow shows why new people discover the app. The retention flow shows why they come back. The depth flow shows how a returning user goes deeper through the Journal.

photo_camera

Flow 1: Snap & Share (Hero Flow)

Dave (Weekend Ritualist) is on his back porch on a Saturday evening with a cigar. He wants to capture the moment and share it to his Facebook cigar group.
1
Welcome Screen (first open only)
On his very first open, Dave sees the app identity and three short statements about what Vitolero does. He taps "Get Started." The camera permission dialog appears. This screen never shows again.
2
Camera State
Camera is active. He points it at the cigar in his hand and taps the shutter.
3
Processing State
A brief narrative streams while the brand and line resolve in under 10 seconds. A cancel pill is visible throughout, so Dave can return to the camera if he framed the wrong cigar.
4
Identification Result
"Padron 1926 No. 9 Maduro." Brand and line, a flavor summary, and a strength indicator. A "Details" button is available for the full Cigar Detail screen. Dave ignores it for now.
5
Filter Preview State
A soft peripheral blur isolates the cigar while the background softens. A brand strip appears at the bottom. Dave picks one of the 3 filters. Tapping the image would toggle the overlay off.
6
Share Card State
Dave taps "Share to Facebook." The deep link opens the share dialog. His group sees the branded photo with the Vitolero domain under the image. Three members ask where the card came from.
7
Return
The photo is saved to Dave's Journal. He never created an account. A PWA install prompt appears as a bottom-sheet card, offering to add Vitolero to his home screen.

Why this flow matters

Dave shared because the photo looked great, not because the app told him to. Three friends now know about the app. The identification happened as a byproduct of behavior Dave already does. If this flow is not effortless, or if the filtered photo does not look impressive enough to share, the product has not worked.

replay

Flow 2: Reopen, Continue, Pair (Retention Flow)

Dave opens the app later that same evening. He wants to see what he was smoking and find something to pair with the bourbon he just poured.
1
Returning User State
The app opens to the last photo taken, not the camera. The Padron 1926 is on screen with action buttons available.
2
Tap Pairing
Dave taps the pairing button. The app generates 6 suggestions based on the Padron 1926 line.
3
Pairing Result
3 food and 3 drink options listed beside the cigar. "Maker's Mark Bourbon" is the featured pairing: "The chocolate and leather notes in the 1926 complement the vanilla and caramel in Maker's Mark."
4
Journal
Dave taps the Journal icon. A sign-in sheet appears with Google and Apple ID options. He taps one. His photo is now saved to his account.

Why this flow matters

Dave returned without a prompt or notification. The value was immediate. The pairing gave him something useful for the moment he was already in. The Journal trigger created the account with minimal friction. This is how casual use becomes regular use.

menu_book

Flow 3: Journal to Cigar Detail (Depth Flow)

Dave opens the app a few days later. He is curious about the tobacco composition of the Padron 1926 he smoked.
1
Last Photo State
The app opens to the last photo taken. Dave taps the Journal pip in the top-right corner.
2
Journal State
The masonry gallery shows his recent cigars. He taps the Padron 1926 card.
3
Cigar Detail Screen
Brand logo, line, wrapper (Maduro), binder, filler, strength indicator, smoke time, flavour chips. He taps "+ more" to expand the full flavour cluster inline, then reads the description paragraph.
4
Back
Dave taps the back arrow, labelled "Journal." He returns to the masonry gallery.

Why this flow matters

The Journal is not just a photo album. It is the entry point to the depth layer. The Cigar Detail screen gives Carlos-type users what they came for without cluttering the share flow that Dave needs. One app, two depths, no conflict.

Why these three flows

The hero flow shows the growth engine: every snap becomes a great-looking share, every share becomes distribution. The retention flow shows why Dave comes back: immediate value with no prompt. The depth flow shows how the Journal opens into Cigar Detail for users who want more. Camera in, share out, the Journal holds the history, and depth is one tap away.

Note on onboarding

There is no separate onboarding sequence. A one-time welcome screen sets context before the camera permission, then the first snap IS onboarding. Value arrives before any account, wizard, or quiz.
