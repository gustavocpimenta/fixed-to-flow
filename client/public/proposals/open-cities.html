Open Cities

From vision to flow

June 2026

Context

Open Cities is launching its first program with the vision clear and the system to run it still missing. Decisions, processes, and context live in the founders' heads and scattered across Google Drive, Slack, WhatsApp, and Film Freeway. A small team across four cities is about to need a single place to know where things are without asking. External funders are about to need traceable, structured reporting. And the team already reaches for AI, each person their own way, with none of it shared.

The moment matters. What gets set early, while the team is small and the pace still allows real thinking, holds its shape as the program grows. This is the window to lay strong principles and a base that grows with the work.

The Challenge

Open Cities has a clear vision on governance. They are clear on who decides, who gives input, who needs to be aligned, and who is informed. The intention is there. What never followed is the operations layer that carries it into daily work. Governance without operations stays on paper.

So everything keeps running through the founders. Legal documentation, Banco de Fomento, investors, participant agreements, mentor recruitment, program coordination. A small founding team, many threads, and information scattered across Google Drive, Slack, WhatsApp, and Film Freeway.

The tooling tells the same story. Someone tried to set Notion up a month ago and it stalled. It sits there still, without the structure a professional approach brings, and the program is starting.

This challenge lives on four levels:

  • Governance. Take the decision grid from the retreat as the starting point and turn it into something that flows, articulated with strong operations for an AI world.
  • Operational. A single hub where information lives, decisions are recorded, and every team member knows where things are without having to ask.
  • Tracking. Banco de Fomento and investors require structured reporting, traceable decisions, and checks-and-balances. The system produces this as a view of work that already exists.
  • Context as asset. The program will generate rare knowledge about filmmakers, technologies, mentors, and creative processes. Captured with intention, that context can become a product. It also becomes an asset for everyday operations and governance: the team talks directly with its own context layer, and that changes how the organization works.

Our Method

Understand before building. Map the reality, listen to the people, understand where time is spent and where the value sits before any implementation decision.

Decide with criteria. Have clarity on what matters for the organization, criteria for what goes in and what stays out, and the capacity to test small before committing the team.

Enable the team in parallel. Enablement happens at the same time as the build, integrated from day one. The work ends with the team knowing how to operate the system autonomously, with no dependency on an external operator.

The Approach

The work splits into two tracks built to move in parallel and feed each other. Track 1, Operations and Systems, builds the spine: governance that flows, Notion as the single hub, and the program captured as living context. Track 2, AI Enablement, brings the team into that system and makes AI a shared layer the whole team draws on, adopted responsibly and with clear judgment about where it belongs. As they advance together, the line between them blurs: stronger operations give AI more to work with, and AI makes the operations lighter to run.

Track 1

Operations and Systems

Governance: from the grid to daily practice

There is already a spark to build on. The decision grid made during the company retreat is a real starting point, and it already expresses the founders' point of view on how decisions should run. What is missing is the next step: translating it into concrete processes the team uses, that satisfy external reporting requirements, and that do not create unnecessary bureaucracy.

The approach is straightforward: every decision has an owner. Everyone knows their role. Processes are designed with the core team, tested during a trial period, and adjusted based on what does not work.

For Open Cities, governance carries an additional layer: the requirements of Banco de Fomento and the investors. The decision system has to produce traceability and checks-and-balances without turning operations into a bureaucracy machine. Both objectives can coexist, as long as the architecture is designed with both in mind from the beginning.

What this includes:

  • Work with the team to operationalize the existing decision grid into clear, actionable processes
  • Create decision flows that serve both internal coordination and external reporting requirements simultaneously
  • Define a trial period with a built-in review to adjust what is not working
  • Deliverable: a governance document of 2-3 pages, with clear, actionable processes, suitable for sharing with investors and funders
Return on investment

The governance designed now with a small team costs far less than the one retrofitted after the program is running at full speed. Every decision captured correctly from the start is also Banco de Fomento reporting that does not need to be reconstructed at the end of the quarter.

Notion: the single hub

Notion has been identified as the solution. The question is what gets built there and in what sequence.

When we help a business take off, we work with a simple principle: just enough operations, just enough governance, and then expand from there. Enough structure to solve the immediate problem without creating overhead nobody will maintain. A small, distributed team needs a system that everyone can use the day after the setup session. Minimum viable structure, with room to grow.

Notion works as a connected system with AI and automation built in. The Business plan includes Notion AI at no extra cost: summaries, search by meaning across everything, meeting transcription, and answers drawn straight from the workspace. Automation carries the routine: a status change notifies the right person, a recorded decision flows into the report, a recurring deadline appears on its own. The databases hold the information, the AI makes sense of it, and the automation keeps it moving.

The exact shape gets decided with the team. As a starting point, a system like this can hold:

  • Program hub. The cohort, the selected filmmakers, the status of each project, the sessions, the mentors. Film Freeway manages applications; Notion manages the program after selection.
  • Relationship management. Investors, partners, mentors, funding entities. Interaction history, follow-ups, context for every meeting.
  • Tasks and projects. Who is doing what, by when, across the program and the organization. Notion setup, mentor recruitment, participant agreements, reporting deadlines, each with an owner and a due date.
  • Decision log. Every relevant decision captured with context: what was decided, when, by whom, and why. Structured reporting with one click.
  • Knowledge base. Governance, policies, processes, references. The place where institutional knowledge lives and depends on nobody's memory.
  • Meeting notes. Templates with assigned actions and clear follow-up. Automatic transcription turns into tasks without manual work.

These pieces do not sit in isolation. They are connected databases that talk to each other: a decision links to the meeting where it was made, a task links to the project and the person it serves, a filmmaker links to their sessions and their mentors. Update something once and it updates everywhere it matters. The AI reads across all of it, and the automation acts on it. That is what turns a set of pages into a system.

Return on investment

A distributed team operating from a single hub stops losing context between timezones and conversations. Coordination sits on a shared system that holds the context the whole team needs. External reporting requirements become a view of what already exists, ready when the quarter closes.

Claude: the accelerator next to the hub

Notion holds the information. Some of the work, though, lives outside it: a funding document to draft, a spreadsheet of applications to make sense of, a contract to review, a question that needs real research before a decision. That is where Claude comes in. It does not replace Notion and it is not the center of the system. It is the accelerator for file work and intensive thinking that a database is not built for.

The two reinforce each other. Claude connects to the Notion workspace, so the context the team captures there becomes something Claude can draw on, and the output of a Claude session lands back in Notion as structured, shared knowledge. Notion AI for what lives in Notion, Claude with Cowork for documents, spreadsheets, and deep work.

Context engineering: capturing the program as an asset

This is the highest-value layer that nobody talks about until it is missing.

Open Cities' inaugural program will generate knowledge that very likely no other organization will have: how specific independent filmmakers work with AI, which creative processes work with which technologies, what mentors say off the record, what the technology company partners bring that is different. That knowledge exists in potential. The question is whether it remains scattered across emails and individual memory, or is captured with intention from the start.

Context engineering, here, is capturing decisions, rationale, and learnings with the right architecture, so that the same information is used everywhere, nothing is lost, and what the program produces can eventually become a product.

In practice:

  • Every program decision captured with context: what was decided, why, what alternatives were considered
  • Every cohort session with a record: what worked, what changed, why
  • Mentor and technology company learnings organized in an accessible, reusable way
  • An architecture that allows the knowledge accumulated in one edition to feed the next

Once this context layer exists, the team can talk to it directly: ask what was decided and why, what worked in a past session, what a mentor recommended, and get an answer grounded in the program's own records. The same shift the connected system brings to daily operations, now applied to everything the program learns.

Notion is where this lives. What Fixed to Flow brings is the discipline of capturing it with the right architecture from the start, so the context stays clean enough to build on and, for an organization generating the kind of knowledge Open Cities will generate, valuable enough to eventually become a product.

Return on investment

The knowledge the program produces stops being ephemeral. It is captured in an architecture that grows edition by edition, that can be the foundation of a future product, and that makes Open Cities an organization that learns in a structured way about what it does.

Track 2

AI Enablement

From individual use to shared context layer

The team already uses AI. ChatGPT, Claude, a Dobbin enterprise assistant connected to Slack that never really got adopted. The pattern is the same as almost everywhere: each person uses it their own way, context is not shared, the work is not replicable.

The shift in this track is from individual use to a shared context layer. The knowledge gained in one AI conversation becomes available for the next, and for other team members. Instructions written once can be used a hundred times. A shared project means context lives with the team and is accessible to everyone.

Enablement organizes across two moments:

Group session with the whole team. Hands-on literacy, worked live in Claude with real Open Cities material. Shared vocabulary, calibrated expectations, and first results in the same session: a funding document drafted with Cowork, a spreadsheet of applications read in minutes, a shared Project so the context the team builds is available to everyone instead of trapped in one person's chat history. The focus stays on the ways of working AI makes possible: reusable context across conversations, work that does not depend on one person, and a shared layer the whole team draws on.

One-on-one mentoring for three people: the two founders and one more team member. Personal sessions tuned to each person's real work, with the Amplify AND Protect framework as the lens throughout: AI amplifies what is routine, human judgment leads where there is relationship, vision, and creative criteria. For the founders, much of the organization lives in their heads, and the mentoring amplifies their capacity directly: using Claude to synthesize, prepare, draft, and decide faster. A partnership memo turned around in an afternoon, a board update pulled together from scattered notes, so response capacity grows without growing headcount. For the third person, the work starts from their specific role: where time goes, what makes sense to delegate, and a practical plan for using Claude in their day-to-day.

Return on investment

The team gets amplified. Each person feels in charge of their work, the founders gain capacity without hiring, and what once lived in scattered threads now flows through one place everyone trusts.

Ethical & Responsible AI

Open Cities has already drawn its own line: the artist's hand. Creative integrity and the human relationship with filmmakers are central to what the organization stands for. Responsible AI use is part of Track 2 precisely because that principle deserves to live in daily practice, shaped by the people doing the work.

In recent years the field has worked through the same question, and the principles are now reasonably settled: consent, disclosure by default, human authorship kept central. The film-specific risks are concrete: a filmmaker's unreleased material surfacing through a consumer AI tool, confidential investor information entering a conversation it was never meant to enter. Recoverable in the technical sense, and far harder to recover from in the relational one.

A note on expertise

We are not filmmakers, and we count on Open Cities' domain knowledge. What we bring is a clear read of where AI has landed and a structured way to turn it into the organization's own framework.

What this includes:

  • A working responsible AI framework developed with the Open Cities team, anchored to the organization's mission, program structure, and creative context.
  • Shared clarity across roles: where AI fits, what warrants care, and the reasoning that makes those lines durable as tools evolve.
  • A transparency principle for when and how AI touches a filmmaker's work, a communication, or a selection process.
  • A built-in intention to revisit the framework as the program and tools develop, on a rhythm that makes sense for the team.
Return on investment

A shared AI framework builds internal trust and gives the team a reliable basis for adopting new tools. People make better calls when they know the principles are already worked out. For an organization with filmmakers, mentors, and partners in the mix, that clarity is in place before any difficult situation arises.

What flow looks like

Picture the team a few months in. Someone in Porto opens Notion in the morning and sees exactly where every project stands, with no message to anyone. A founder asks the system what was decided about a partnership and why, and reads the answer in seconds. When Banco de Fomento asks for an update, the report is a view that already exists, ready in minutes. The second cohort starts, and everything the first one taught is already there to build on. AI handles the first pass, and the team brings the judgment only people have.

That is the state this work moves toward: a system that carries the weight the founders carry today, so the people are free to do the work only they can do.
