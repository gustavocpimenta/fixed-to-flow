Context & Situation

You're at a fascinating point in your journey.

You've got a neuroscience degree from King's College London, but you've discovered that the microscopic approach doesn't call to you. You're a big-picture thinker. You care about people and impact. You're doing digital marketing for your parents' consultancy and Impact Hub Lusaka, creating posters, invitations, videos. You're a facilitator. And you're in that transition space where you're figuring out your direction.

That takes courage. Not everyone has the honesty to say "this isn't quite right" and actively explore what fits better.

Your father Martin referred you, but what stood out in our conversation was your curiosity. You asked questions most people don't ask in a first call. You asked about ethical AI. You wondered about "brain rust." You asked if this work would make you better or just faster.

Those questions matter. They tell me you're thinking clearly about what you want from AI.

The Challenge

You're in that uncomfortable space where you can see AI's potential but you're not sure how to make it work for you. You've got multiple interests pulling you in different directions:

  • Research proposals and funding applications for Impact Hub
  • Training AI to write in your voice and persona
  • Exploring app creation (like your friend's fitness planner)
  • Maintaining your knowledge base across neuroscience and marketing
  • Building facilitation skills with AI-powered tools

Right now, AI feels generic. You ask it things, it gives you generic answers. But you know there's a better way. You want AI that actually knows you. Your voice. Your context. Your goals.

You've got something precious: time. 1-3 hours a day. Most people don't have that kind of space to build something meaningful with AI. But time isn't enough. You need structure, good experiments, and accountability.

Brain rust is real. If you hand off all your thinking to AI without staying in the loop, you will lose sharpness. But if you do this right, AI becomes an amplifier for what you already do well.

The Approach

I use personal experiments. That's how I learned GenAI in 2023, and it's the method I use with everyone I work with.

A personal experiment is simple: pick one specific thing you want AI to help with. Design a clear test. Try it for a defined period. See what happens. Reflect on whether it made you better or just faster.

No abstract theory. Real work. Real results. Real learning.

How we'll work:

  • Setup session (90 minutes). We cover AI fundamentals, discuss your goals, and build your first assistant together. Something real you can use immediately.
  • Ongoing sessions. We run experiments across your use cases: research systems, AI persona training, funding proposals, maybe vibe coding if your friend's fitness project is still alive.
  • Between sessions. You work on your experiments. When you hit a wall or have a breakthrough, we talk about it.

I'm not here to give you a list of prompts. I'm here to help you develop AI fluency. That's the difference between tool training and real capability.

And because you've got that curiosity about ethics and cognition, we can go deeper. We can talk about when to trust AI and when to trust yourself. We can design guardrails so you stay sharp while AI handles the grunt work.
