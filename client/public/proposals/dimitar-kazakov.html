Designing the UX concept for an AI-first cigar recommendation app

February 2026

Context & Situation

Dimitar, it's good to reconnect after all these years. You're building something ambitious and technically sophisticated. Close to a year of work on a cigar recommendation app with real intelligence behind it.

You've built the foundation. LLM backend that understands cigars. Comprehensive database with images, flavors, ratings, pricing, origins. A working prototype already in your hands. You moved from a native app approach (too deterministic, overcomplicated) to a progressive web app. The technology works.

What's missing is the conceptual UX layer. How to organize this intelligence so it feels natural to use. How to present rich information without overloading people. How to make an AI-first interface intuitive for different types of people who smoke cigars for different reasons. That's why you reached out.

You have the rare combination problem. Deep technical knowledge, years of product experience, clear vision for radical simplicity (photo + voice/text = done). But you need someone who has both deep UX experience and practical AI knowledge to design the interaction model.

As you said on our call, you want to compensate me fairly for my brain. I appreciate that directness. Let's figure this out together.

The Challenge

You're facing three core tensions.

Intelligence without interface.

You've built the intelligence layer. Your AI knows cigars better than most experts. It has the data, the training, the sophistication to give real recommendations. But intelligence without the right interface is like a sommelier who can only communicate through a terminal window.

The challenge is designing an interaction model that makes that knowledge feel natural and effortless. Where users don't think about the AI. They just ask, and it knows.

The future vs the present.

Your vision is radical simplicity. Photo + voice/text = actionable response. No menus. No buttons. No screens to learn. That IS the future. But most users aren't there yet. They're used to interfaces that hold their hand. They expect structure, even when the technology doesn't need it.

The challenge is designing an experience that guides those users into this new way of interacting without falling back on the old patterns. Meeting people where they are while moving them toward the vision, not away from it.

Different people, different jobs.

Not everyone smokes cigars for the same reason. Some want flavor exploration. Others want social currency (what to bring to that dinner). Some want value for money. Others want ritual and relaxation.

The app needs to recognize these different motivations and respond accordingly, without asking 20 questions upfront. You learned this the hard way with the first version: every question costs engagement. The interaction model needs to sense intent and deliver value immediately. Then progressively deepen the relationship. That requires careful UX thinking about context, defaults, and progressive engagement.

This is where UX stops being about screens and buttons, and becomes about designing the bridge between how LLMs think and how humans think.

The Approach

This is a working partnership, not a deliverable dump. Three phases. The exact output format gets determined during discovery. This is intentional. The right deliverable depends on the problem.

Phase 1: Discovery and mapping

  • Deep dive into the product, the users, and the constraints
  • Map the different user types and their "jobs to be done"
  • Understand what the LLM can and can't do today
  • Identify the key moments where UX decisions will make or break the experience

This phase is about getting aligned on the problem before solving it. I need to understand your technology, your users, and your constraints. You need to see how I think. Each session comes with a preparation brief so we make the most of our time together.

Phase 2: Concept design

  • Define the interaction model: how users communicate with the AI and how the AI responds
  • Design the core user flows (onboarding, in-store recommendation, drink pairing, whatever the priority scenarios are)
  • Establish design principles specific to this product

The exact format of the deliverable (concept document, annotated flows, pattern library, or a combination) will be determined based on what we learn in Phase 1. This is intentional. The right output depends on what you and your team need to execute without me in the room.

Phase 3: Review and handoff

  • Iterate based on feedback
  • Prepare clear guidance for your team to execute
  • Make sure the concept can be built without me in the room

You shouldn't need me for every decision. The concept should give your team the structure to move forward.
